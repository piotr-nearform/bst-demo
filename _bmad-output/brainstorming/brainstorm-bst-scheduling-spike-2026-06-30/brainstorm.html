02Technique · Role Playing

Four voices around one PR

Run the engagement's tensions as a conversation — the skeptic, the product owner, the engineer, the delivery lead.

K
KPMG skeptic

How do I trust code an AI wrote? Where does accountability live when the machine does the typing?

PO
Product Owner

The PR description stays simple and compact: it restates the problem and the acceptance criteria from the ticket, then maps exactly which tests cover each AC. The PRD lives in version control, so the decision trail is visible too.

EN
Engineer

Good testing makes the PR readable — the test evidence is the product, it needs to read well. The reviewer focuses on the tests, skims the implementation, and watches the sensitive files: package.json, infra, dependencies.

DL
Delivery Lead

A human leaves the final approval and owns it — that's more accountability than today. And on velocity: structured right, the product mostly builds itself and we're mostly observers. Slower than unsupervised AI, sure — that's not the bar — but requirements and dev cycles both get much faster, and with the right automation we power through the stories.

03Technique · One Feature Only

● The double-booking guard Assign a person to a job over a date range; the system refuses any assignment that clashes with an existing one. Non-trivial because of concurrency: two planners booking the same person mid-flight must be handled as a race/conflict — not a naive read-then-check. That's what lets the spike showcase how we work, not trivial CRUD.
✕Notifications & approvals workflow

Synthesis · the four keystones

Where the four techniques meet

Each thread collapsed into the next. Four insights, then one direction.

01

Engineering hygiene is the sales pitch

A readable, test-first PR is both the dev practice and the KPMG trust demo — the same artifact does both jobs at once.

02

The clash forces the conversation

The concurrent-clash edge is what forces the PO ↔ engineer spec negotiation. Choosing the feature and choosing the multi-role showcase are one decision.

One constraint pays three times: accountability (a human can genuinely review and own it), velocity (review stays cheap, so it doesn't eat the AI speed gains), and reviewability.
